Research Project
Miro-E

Miro-E is a small, programmable robot designed for educational and research purposes.

It features sensors for perception, can be programmed to exhibit various behaviors like

locomotion and social interaction, and offers a platform for learning about robotics and

artificial intelligence. Its compact design, modularity, and programmability make it suitable

for a wide range of applications in schools, universities, and research institutions.

User Testing

Post scenarios we were ready to build the behaviour of MIRO-E.

We employed the Sense-Think-Act model for an interactive system

We choose two variables to test out how interaction would take place

-Placement of Miro

-Activity level of miro-E

 

 

Based on the feedback of actors we proceeded with Behaviour design based on

appropriate activiy level and placement

Based on all the scenes played out and the commentary of the actors itself, specifically

when actor played the child, helped to better understand what type of behavior and how the

height of the robot influenced the comfort a child could possibly feel from the robot. When it comes

to what type of activity level of the robot is ideal, the robot should not be too active for it results in

the child becoming too active and energetic. So, a balance between the low and medium activity of

the robot should be found, in which there will be some interaction especially when the child becomes

a bit too stressed as way of providing a distraction/comfort. But it should not be too active that it wakes

up the child and demands attention when a child clearly just wants to relax. So smooth and slow

movements would possibly work best.

Setting Up Participants & Consent Summary & Conclusion

Based on all the scenes played out and the commentary of the actors itself, specifically when actor

played the child, helped to better understand what type of behavior and how the height of the robot

influenced the comfort a child could possibly feel from the robot. When it comes down to what type of

activity level of the robot is ideal, the robot should not be too active for it results in the child becoming

too active and energetic. So, a balance between the low and medium activity of the robot should be

found, in which there will be some interaction especially when the child becomes a bit too stressed as

way of providing a distraction/comfort. But it should not be too active that it wakes up the child and

demands attention when a child clearly just wants to relax. So smooth and slow movements would

possibly work best.

When it comes to the second changing parameter we tested, the height of the robot, lead to interesting

and some surprising conclusions. Starting with that placing the robot on the ground, leads too some

uncomfortable situations for the child if it wants to see the robot. When the robot is in a higher position,

the child easily falls back asleep for the robot is so ‘far away’ that there is barely any interaction happening.

In this type of situation, the robot provides comfort, like the way that stars are placed on the ceiling in

children’s room, it’s a distant type of comfort but it also felt a bit threatening to the child, which of course

is a very undesirable course. For a more interactive type of comfort the robot should be placed within

arms reach or in a way that allows the robot more freedom in movement.

A note on the insights from this improv theatre experiment is that these actors all have had their own

experiences which can influence their view on things and how they perceive some behavior. This means

that these one-time experiments are unique to these actors and cannot give us an insight into the

experience of the general public or our specific target user.

All in all, this embodied improv theatre test provided a great insight into how a child would feel and

how both the activity level of the robot and the position of the robot change the atmosphere and the

interaction with the child.

There will be roughly asked 3 – 5 participants asked to participate. Each participate will is a student

also taking the ‘Social Robot Design’- course. Meaning all participants will have a level of bias during

the answering and interacting with the Miro-E. The students will also be familiar with the Miro-E.

The setting of the user test is as follows:

The participant will be asked for spoken agreement for gathering anonymized data. Before starting with

the first scenario, the participant is asked how they are feeling currently, in the emotional context.

The participant will be sat in front of a table with the Miro-E and are asked to interact with it. The Miro-E

will behave according to one of the three states after which the participant is asked the following four

questions:

  1. How did you interpret the Miro-E behavior? (Likert-scale question between 1 – 7)

o Happy – Sad

o Engaged – disengaged

o Interested – disinterested  2. How did you feel?

  1. Did you feel comforted/understood or not at all?

Afterwards all three sessions the participant is asked to answer one final question: -

  1. Which of the three scenarios brought you most comfort?
